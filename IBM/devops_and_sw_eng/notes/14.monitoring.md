## Monitoring and Observability for Development and DevOps

### Monitoring Basics

- Monitoring is for the identification, measurement, and evaluation of applications.
- Monitoring results in an application that is performant and valuable.
- There are many types of monitoring tools, and one type or a combination of types can be used to give you maximum visibility into your software and its performance.
- Monitoring types focus on the many functions of IT systems and infrastructure.
- The four Golden Signals of Monitoring are latency, traffic, errors, and saturation.
- The Golden Signals help you focus on the most critical performance indicators in your applications and systems.

### Objectives of Monitoring

- Monitoring an application is an ongoing process, whereas evaluation of an application may happen only a few times.
- Monitoring ensures that applications are performing well and as expected. Evaluation shows that an application is both relevant and profitable.
- Metrics, observation, and alerting are all essential components that together form the basis of a monitoring system.
- Ideal monitoring systems have an independent infrastructure, easy-to-use and reliable systems, maintained historical data, and effective correlation of data from different sources.
- Important metrics you should track are host-based, application, network and connectivity, and server pool metrics.
- Factors that help you determine which metrics you should monitor include the resources available for tracking, your application's complexity, and how complex your deployment environment is.
- Application monitoring with application performance monitoring (APM) tools ensures real-time measuring of performance and efficiency.
- Important monitoring elements include various platforms like devices and operating systems, complex development processes like repeated application testing, and business continuity.

- Synthetic monitoring is used to track how well an application or website performs.
- Synthetic monitoring is important as it provides quicker problem resolution, alerting, moderating other content, SLA compliance, and monitors complex transactions and business processes.

### Logging

- Application logging provides a logged record of the application's activities
- An application log records information about events that have occurred in an application.
- Some things to consider logging are an application's incoming and outgoing messages, services and functions, business cases and user journeys, data operations, system events, performance statistics, and threats and vulnerabilities.
- Log monitoring software is built to perform essential event log monitoring tasks consistently and accurately.
- These tools allow you to generate alerts and reports to help you stay on top of log monitoring and create clear visualizations for at-a-glance insights into network performance.
- There are recommended logging tools, such as LogDNA, Sumo Logic, Mezmo, Instana, and Datadog.
- Distributed logging is a technique used in computing systems to collect and store log data from multiple sources across different nodes or servers.
- Distributed tracing is the practice of tracking a single request or transaction as it moves through a distributed system.
- Important concepts in distributed tracing include trace, span, trace ID, parent/child relation, context propagation, and instrumentation.

### Logging Implementation

- Common application logs are event, error, access, performance, and debugging logs.
- Most logging frameworks share common features regarding levels, log messages, and destinations.
- Structured logging enables standardized formats for effortless parsing, querying, and analytics.
- A log management system must parse the files to extract meaningful information from logs.
- Log parsers are built into the log management software’s engine.
- Monitoring and analyzing logs enhance the observability of the network, providing transparency and visibility.
- Storing log data is required for different reasons, including the reliability of systems, the security posture of environments, improvement in IT systems’ decision-making, and auditing purposes.
- Visualizing the log data spanning days or weeks is necessary to gain insights into an app’s behavior and performance changes over time.
- Storing logs in the cloud enables scalable storage capacity that aligns with log data needs.
- You can back up data to on-premises servers or in the cloud for storing logs.
- A few analytical dimensions that give a relative idea of how long the retention period should be criticality, security, maturity, frequency, cost-effectiveness, and discovery and resolution.
- It is important to consider the best practices for storing logs.

### Observability

- Observability is a term used in engineering and computer science to describe the ability to understand the internal state of a system using its external outputs.
- Observability offers powerful benefits to IT teams, organizations, application developers, and users, such as application performance monitoring, infrastructure and cloud monitoring, and user experience.
- The three pillars of observability are logs, metrics, and traces.
- Logs capture detailed information about individual events or transactions, giving you a chronological record of what happened.
- Metrics are numerical measurements with accompanying attributes that show the health of a particular system component.
- Traces are records of the information pathways or workflows that are created to follow a work item, like a transaction.
- Cloud native observability is the practice of monitoring and understanding the behavior of cloud-native applications running in dynamic and distributed environments.
- The pillars of cloud enterprise observability are automation, context, and intelligent action.
- Cloud native observability tools and solutions are critical for organizations and can correlate telemetry data across cloud environments at a massive scale.
- Popular cloud native observability tools: Prometheus, Jaeger, FluentD, Thanos, Datadog, New Relic, AWS CloudWatch, Google Cloud Monitoring, Instana, and Mezmo.
- Sampling in logging is the practice of collecting only a subset of log events for analysis or storage. Instead of logging every single event or piece of data, a subset is selected randomly or by some other criteria for recording.
- Advantages of sampling: Reduced overhead, enhanced performance, cost-effectiveness, improved accuracy, and better scalability.
- Disadvantages of sampling: Missing details, inaccurate data, limited resolution, masked outliers, and challenges in diagnosing complex performance issues.
- IBM Instana Observability, popularly known as Instana, is a fully automated application performance management (APM) solution specially designed to overcome the challenges of managing microservice and cloud-native applications.
- Instana monitors and analyzes your applications, services, infrastructure, web browsers, mobile applications, and more for over 200 domain-specific technologies.
- Instana automates dependency mapping across the full stack and informs when customers are impacted by performance or stability issues.

### Tracing using Open Telemetry

- Telemetry is the automated process of collecting and transmitting data from remote or inaccessible sources to support monitoring and analysis.
- Telemetry is essential for developers to spot application errors and crashes, analyze performance metrics, use analytics, and monitor security events.
- The key benefits of telemetry include remote feedback, real-time monitoring of application performance, user activity and experience, and network analytics and security.
- Distributed tracing is a technique of observing requests transmitted through distributed cloud environments.
- Popular telemetry tools: Datadog, Dynatrace, New Relic, Sumo Logic, Splunk, and Instana.
- Popular distributed tracing tools: Atatus, SigNoz, Jaeger, Zipkin, HoneyComb, New Relic, and Dynatrace.

### Monitoring Containers

- Tracing requests across multiple containers and services allows you to quickly identify the component causing latency or errors and resolve issues more efficiently.
- There are five steps to implement tracing for container-based applications: Choose a tracing tool, instrument your code, configure the tracing agent, deploy the tracer, and finally, verify functionality.
- For optimal performance and reliability of your system, implement end-to-end instrumentation and record traces for all inbound and outbound service calls.
